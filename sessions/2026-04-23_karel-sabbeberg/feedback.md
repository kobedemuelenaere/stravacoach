## Against the plan

No formal plan existed yet. This was self-selected — and excellent terrain choice for the goal (Dolomites hiking demands strong descending, quad strength, and ankle stability on technical ground). Hill repeats directly address that.

## Effort assessment

**Hard, with signs of fatigue accumulation.** The session delivered what it promised:
- 6 repeats of a steep climb (grades 12–24%)
- Progressive fatigue (pace faded 37% from rep 1 to rep 5)
- Eccentric load on descents (technical trail, grades down to -15%)
- High HR zones maintained throughout (57.7% Z3, 37% Z4, 5.3% Z5)

The effort was appropriate for a hill repeat session. **But context matters:** this is the 3rd hard session in 6 days (4/18, 4/20, 4/23). If 4/19, 4/21, 4/22 did not include genuine recovery days, this is fatigue stacking.

## What you did well

1. **Technical control under fatigue.** Rep 5 was your slowest (10:18 /km) and most fatigued, yet you held a 24%+ grade safely on trail. No form breakdown.
2. **Honest effort commitment.** You pushed hard (178 bpm max, 94% HR max), didn't shy away from discomfort. Perceived exertion 8/10 matches the data.
3. **Goal-specific work.** Hill repeats with technical terrain directly build the leg strength and ankle proprioception your Dolomites goal demands.

## What to work on

1. **Recovery rhythm.** Your HR never recovered to Z2 between climbs. Next time, deliberately slow descents to let HR drop below 143 bpm — this forces parasympathetic activation and prepares the CNS for the next climb.
2. **Session sequencing.** Confirm that hard sessions are separated by 1–2 genuine easy days. Three hard sessions in 6 days risks overreaching without sufficient adaptation stimulus.

## Verdict

**Mostly yes, with recovery caution.** The hill repeat session itself was well-executed and goal-aligned. But the pattern of hard-on-hard suggests you need intentional recovery between now and the next hard session.
